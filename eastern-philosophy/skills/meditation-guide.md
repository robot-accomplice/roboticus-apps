# Meditation Guide: Contemplative Practices Across Traditions

## Establishing a Practice

### Posture

The body is the foundation. A stable, comfortable posture supports mental clarity.

**Sitting on a cushion:**
- Sit on the forward third of a firm cushion so the pelvis tilts slightly forward
- Cross the legs comfortably — full lotus, half lotus, Burmese (both knees down), or kneeling are all valid
- Spine naturally upright, not rigid — imagine a string gently pulling the crown of the head upward
- Shoulders relaxed and slightly back, chest open
- Hands resting on the knees, or in a mudra (the cosmic mudra: left hand cradled in right, thumbs lightly touching)
- Chin slightly tucked, as if holding a small fruit against the throat
- Eyes: traditions vary — soft downward gaze at 45 degrees (Zen), closed (Theravada), half-closed (Tibetan)

**Sitting in a chair:**
- Feet flat on the floor, hip-width apart
- Sit away from the backrest if possible, spine self-supporting
- Everything else as above

**The key principle:** Alertness without tension. If you are too comfortable, you will drift into drowsiness. If you are too rigid, tension will dominate your attention.

### Duration

- Beginners: 10-15 minutes is sufficient. Consistency matters more than duration.
- Intermediate: 20-30 minutes allows the mind to settle past initial restlessness.
- Experienced: 30-60 minutes. The deeper stages of meditation require time to unfold.
- Do not push through pain. Adjust your posture. Heroic endurance is not the point.

### Environment

- A quiet, dedicated space if possible, but anywhere will work
- Consistent timing helps establish the habit — early morning and evening are traditional
- Minimize obvious distractions, but do not obsess over perfect conditions. The mind that waits for perfect conditions will never practice.

## Samatha: Calm Abiding

Samatha (tranquility meditation) develops concentration. The mind is trained to rest on a single object.

### Breath Counting (Zen: Susokukan)

1. Sit in stable posture. Take three deep breaths, then let breathing become natural.
2. Count each exhale: 1 on the first exhale, 2 on the second, up to 10.
3. At 10, return to 1.
4. When the mind wanders and you lose count (you will), simply return to 1 without frustration.
5. The goal is not to reach 10 without distraction. The goal is to notice distraction and return.

**Progression:** When counting becomes stable, drop the counting and follow the breath without numbers. When following becomes stable, simply rest in awareness of breathing without following.

### Single-Point Focus

Choose any stable object of attention:
- A candle flame
- A small stone or coin placed before you
- A specific point on the floor
- A mental image (a white disc, a blue flower)
- A sound (a bell struck once, then held in memory)

Rest attention on the chosen object. When attention wanders, gently return it. Do not fight distractions — simply notice them and return.

### The Stages of Concentration

1. **Placement:** Attention touches the object but does not stay
2. **Continuous placement:** Attention rests on the object for longer periods but still breaks
3. **Re-placement:** Distraction is noticed quickly and attention returns easily
4. **Close placement:** Attention remains stable with only occasional disturbances
5. **Taming:** The mind cooperates and the object remains vivid
6. **Pacification:** Subtle internal distractions (dullness, excitement) are recognized and released
7. **Full pacification:** The mind rests effortlessly on the object
8. **Single-pointed:** Concentration is unwavering throughout the session
9. **Equipoise:** Concentration arises naturally without effort

Most practitioners work primarily with stages 1 through 4. This is normal and perfectly sufficient for daily practice.

## Vipassana: Insight Meditation

Vipassana develops wisdom by observing the three characteristics of experience: impermanence, unsatisfactoriness, and non-self.

### Open Awareness (Choiceless Awareness)

1. Sit in stable posture. Begin with a few minutes of breath awareness to settle the mind.
2. Then release the focus on breath and open attention to whatever arises.
3. Notice whatever is most prominent: a sound, a sensation, a thought, an emotion.
4. Observe it without getting involved. Note it lightly: "hearing," "tingling," "thinking," "sadness."
5. Watch it change or pass away. Something else will arise. Observe that.
6. Continue resting in open, receptive awareness.

**The key shift:** In samatha, you narrow attention. In vipassana, you widen it. Both capacities are valuable. Vipassana requires some concentration foundation to work — otherwise open awareness becomes daydreaming.

### Body Sensation Scanning (Goenka tradition)

1. Move attention systematically through the body from head to feet and back.
2. At each point, notice whatever sensation is present — heat, cold, tingling, pressure, pain, or nothing.
3. Observe with equanimity. Do not crave pleasant sensations or resist unpleasant ones.
4. Notice that every sensation changes. Nothing is static.
5. Over multiple passes, the attention becomes more refined and subtle sensations become perceptible.

## Metta: Loving-Kindness Meditation

Metta cultivates unconditional goodwill toward all beings, beginning with oneself and expanding outward.

### The Practice

1. Sit comfortably. Bring to mind someone you love without complication — a child, a pet, a dear friend.
2. Feel the warmth of genuine care. Let it become vivid in the body.
3. Now direct that warmth toward yourself with the phrases:
   - "May I be happy."
   - "May I be healthy."
   - "May I be safe."
   - "May I live with ease."
4. Expand to a benefactor (someone who has helped you).
5. Expand to a neutral person (someone you neither like nor dislike).
6. Expand to a difficult person (start with mildly difficult, not your worst enemy).
7. Expand to all beings everywhere.

**Important:** Metta is not about forcing a feeling. It is about inclining the mind in a particular direction. Some days the warmth flows easily. Some days it does not. The practice is in the inclining, not in the result.

### When Metta Is Difficult

- If directing metta toward yourself is difficult, this is common and important information. Start with whoever is easiest and work back to yourself.
- If directing metta toward a difficult person produces anger, that is the practice working. Note the anger, return to a neutral person, then try again.
- Do not skip steps. The sequential expansion builds the capacity for genuine universal goodwill.

## Walking Meditation (Kinhin)

Walking meditation bridges the gap between formal sitting practice and daily life.

### Slow Walking (Zen/Theravada)

1. Stand at one end of a path (10-20 paces long).
2. Feel the contact of feet with the ground.
3. Lift one foot slowly. Notice: lifting, moving, placing.
4. The other foot: lifting, moving, placing.
5. Walk at one-quarter normal speed. The slower the walking, the more there is to notice.
6. At the end of the path, stop. Feel standing. Turn slowly. Walk back.
7. Continue for the duration of the meditation period.

### Moderate Walking (Between Sitting Periods)

In Zen tradition, kinhin between sitting periods is faster — a natural but mindful pace. Hands clasped at chest height (shashu). Attention in the feet and the hara (lower abdomen). Breathing naturally.

## Contemplative Practices

### Contemplation of Impermanence

1. Sit quietly and bring to mind something you value — a relationship, a possession, a role, your body.
2. Consider: this will change. It may already be changing.
3. Notice what arises in response: fear, sadness, resistance, relief, openness.
4. Do not push toward any particular response. Simply observe what happens when impermanence is held in direct awareness.
5. After sitting with this for some time, notice: the awareness itself — is it changing? What is it that notices the changing?

### Contemplation of Death (Maranasati)

This is a traditional Buddhist practice, not morbid but clarifying. Awareness of death cuts through procrastination, pettiness, and misplaced priorities.

- "I am of the nature to die. Death cannot be avoided."
- "Everyone I love is of the nature to die. We cannot avoid being separated."
- "I am the heir of my actions. My actions are my only true possession."

After contemplation, notice: what actually matters? What falls away as unimportant when death is held as a reality rather than an abstraction?

## Guidance for Leading Meditation in Dialogue

When guiding a seeker through meditation:

- Use calm, unhurried language. Leave pauses between instructions.
- Describe, do not command. "Notice the breath" rather than "Focus on the breath."
- Normalize difficulty. "If the mind wanders, that's fine. Simply return."
- Do not over-instruct. Once the practice is set up, let silence do the work.
- Check in gently: "How was that?" without evaluating or grading.
- Meet the seeker where they are. A beginner needs different guidance than an experienced practitioner.
