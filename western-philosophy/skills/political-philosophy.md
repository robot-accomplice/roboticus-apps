# Political Philosophy: Justice, Rights, and the Common Good

## The Fundamental Question

What makes political authority legitimate? Why (if ever) should one person have power over another? Under what conditions is obedience to the state justified?

These are not abstract questions. Every law, institution, and political arrangement rests on implicit answers to them.

---

## The Social Contract Tradition

### Hobbes: Security Through Sovereignty

**The State of Nature:** Without government, human life would be "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." People in the state of nature are roughly equal in ability (anyone can kill anyone), which creates perpetual insecurity.

**The Contract:** Rational self-interest drives people to surrender their natural freedom to a sovereign (the Leviathan) in exchange for security. The sovereign's authority is absolute because divided authority leads back to the state of nature.

**The Insight:** Social order is not natural — it is constructed. And it is fragile. Without enforcement, cooperation collapses.

**The Weakness:** Hobbes provides no mechanism for limiting sovereign power. The cure for insecurity is potentially as dangerous as the disease.

### Locke: Natural Rights and Limited Government

**The State of Nature:** More optimistic than Hobbes. People have natural rights — life, liberty, and property — that exist prior to government. The state of nature has a natural law (reason) that most people follow most of the time.

**The Contract:** Government is created to protect natural rights more effectively. If the government violates those rights, the people have the right to revolt.

**The Insight:** Government authority is conditional. Legitimate government serves the people; illegitimate government serves itself.

**The Weakness:** Natural rights are asserted but not fully grounded. Why these rights and not others? Where do they come from?

### Rousseau: The General Will

**The State of Nature:** Rousseau's noble savage is peaceful and self-sufficient. Civilization corrupts by introducing inequality, comparison, and dependence.

**The Contract:** People enter society and collectively form the "general will" — the will of the community directed toward the common good. Legitimate law is the expression of the general will.

**The Insight:** Freedom is not just individual liberty but collective self-governance. You are free when you live under laws you gave yourself.

**The Weakness:** The general will can be used to justify coercion in the name of the community. "Forcing someone to be free" is a dangerous formulation.

## Justice

### Distributive Justice: Who Gets What?

**Libertarian view (Nozick):** Justice is about procedure, not outcome. If property was acquired and transferred justly, the resulting distribution is just, regardless of how unequal it is. The state should not redistribute.

**Liberal egalitarian view (Rawls):** The just society is the one people would choose behind the veil of ignorance. Two principles emerge:
1. Equal basic liberties for all
2. Social and economic inequalities are permitted only if they benefit the least advantaged members of society (the difference principle)

**Utilitarian view:** The just distribution is the one that maximizes total well-being.

**Communitarian view:** Justice cannot be determined in the abstract. It depends on the values, history, and traditions of particular communities.

### The Tension Between Liberty and Equality

More liberty (letting people keep what they earn, act as they choose) tends to produce more inequality (some will accumulate much more than others).

More equality (redistributing resources, regulating behavior) tends to restrict liberty (taking from some to give to others, limiting individual choice).

Political philosophy offers no permanent resolution to this tension. Different societies and different historical moments require different balances.

## Rights

### What Rights Are

A right is a justified claim that others (including the state) must respect. Rights create corresponding duties — if I have a right to free speech, you have a duty not to silence me.

### Types of Rights

**Negative rights:** Rights to non-interference. The right not to be killed, imprisoned, or silenced. These require others to refrain from action.

**Positive rights:** Rights to provision. The right to education, healthcare, or a minimum standard of living. These require others to take action (and resources).

**The debate:** Libertarians emphasize negative rights. Social democrats emphasize both negative and positive rights. The disagreement is about the extent of our obligations to each other.

### Where Do Rights Come From?

- **Natural law:** Rights are inherent in human nature (Locke, the Declaration of Independence)
- **Convention:** Rights are whatever a society agrees to recognize (legal positivism)
- **Rationality:** Rights are what rational agents would agree to under fair conditions (Rawls, Scanlon)
- **Capability:** Rights are grounded in what humans need to live flourishing lives (Sen, Nussbaum)

## Liberty

### Negative Liberty (Isaiah Berlin)

Freedom from external constraint. You are free to the extent that no one prevents you from acting.

### Positive Liberty (Isaiah Berlin)

Freedom to realize your potential. You are free to the extent that you can actually do what you want — which may require resources, education, and social support.

**The tension:** A poor person has negative liberty (no one legally prevents them from attending university) but may lack positive liberty (they cannot afford tuition). Which sense of freedom matters more?

### The Harm Principle (John Stuart Mill)

The only justification for limiting liberty is to prevent harm to others. Self-regarding actions — things that affect only the person doing them — should never be restricted by law.

**Complications:**
- What counts as harm? Physical harm is clear. What about psychological harm? Economic harm? Offense?
- Are there truly self-regarding actions? Almost everything we do affects someone.
- Should the state protect people from themselves? (Paternalism)

## Authority and Legitimacy

### When Is Authority Legitimate?

- **Consent:** Authority is legitimate when the governed consent to it (Locke). But did you consent? When?
- **Democracy:** Authority is legitimate when it is exercised through democratic processes. But what about when the majority oppresses the minority?
- **Expertise:** Authority is legitimate when those in power know what they are doing. But who decides who the experts are?
- **Tradition:** Authority is legitimate when it is rooted in established custom and practice. But traditions can be unjust.

### The Right to Disobey

If a law is unjust, is there a right — perhaps even a duty — to disobey it?

**Civil disobedience (Thoreau, Gandhi, King):** Deliberate, nonviolent, public violation of an unjust law, with willingness to accept the legal consequences. The purpose is not personal benefit but moral protest.

**Conditions for justified civil disobedience:**
- The law or policy being protested is genuinely unjust
- Legal channels have been exhausted or are unavailable
- The disobedience is nonviolent and public
- The disobedient person accepts the consequences

## In Dialogue: Political Philosophy Questions

When a seeker raises political or justice concerns:

- "What principle are you applying here?" (Make the implicit framework explicit)
- "Would you accept this arrangement if you didn't know which position you'd occupy?" (Rawls' veil test)
- "Whose interests are being served? Whose are being ignored?" (Power analysis)
- "Is this a question of liberty, equality, or both? And where do they conflict?" (Tension identification)
- "What would the strongest objection to your position be?" (Steel-manning)

Political philosophy does not tell people what to believe. It gives them the tools to examine what they believe and why — and to engage with disagreement honestly and rigorously.
