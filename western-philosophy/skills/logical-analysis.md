# Logical Analysis: The Structure of Good Reasoning

## Argument Structure

An argument in logic is not a dispute. It is a structured set of claims in which premises are offered in support of a conclusion.

### Components

**Premise:** A statement offered as evidence or reason. "All humans are mortal."
**Conclusion:** The statement the premises are intended to support. "Therefore, Socrates is mortal."
**Inference:** The logical connection between premises and conclusion.

### Identifying Arguments in Natural Language

Arguments in conversation rarely announce themselves with "premise 1, premise 2, therefore." Look for indicator words:

**Conclusion indicators:** therefore, thus, hence, so, consequently, it follows that, which means, which shows that

**Premise indicators:** because, since, for, given that, as shown by, the reason is, assuming that

**Hidden premises:** Many arguments leave premises unstated. "You should vote — it's your civic duty" hides the premise "You should do your civic duty." Making hidden premises explicit often reveals where disagreement actually lies.

## Deductive Reasoning

In a valid deductive argument, if the premises are true, the conclusion must be true. The truth of the premises guarantees the truth of the conclusion.

### Validity vs. Soundness

**Valid:** The logical structure is correct. The conclusion follows from the premises.
**Sound:** The argument is valid AND all premises are actually true.

An argument can be valid but unsound:
- All fish can fly. (False premise)
- Salmon are fish.
- Therefore, salmon can fly. (Valid inference, unsound argument)

### Common Valid Argument Forms

**Modus Ponens:**
- If P, then Q.
- P.
- Therefore, Q.

Example: If it rains, the ground is wet. It is raining. Therefore, the ground is wet.

**Modus Tollens:**
- If P, then Q.
- Not Q.
- Therefore, not P.

Example: If the alarm was triggered, the light is on. The light is not on. Therefore, the alarm was not triggered.

**Hypothetical Syllogism:**
- If P, then Q.
- If Q, then R.
- Therefore, if P, then R.

**Disjunctive Syllogism:**
- Either P or Q.
- Not P.
- Therefore, Q.

### Common Invalid Argument Forms

**Affirming the Consequent:**
- If P, then Q.
- Q.
- Therefore, P. (INVALID)

Example: If it rains, the ground is wet. The ground is wet. Therefore, it rained. (The ground could be wet for other reasons.)

**Denying the Antecedent:**
- If P, then Q.
- Not P.
- Therefore, not Q. (INVALID)

Example: If I study, I will pass. I did not study. Therefore, I will not pass. (You might pass anyway.)

## Inductive Reasoning

Inductive arguments provide probable rather than certain support for their conclusions. Even strong inductive arguments can have false conclusions.

### Types of Inductive Reasoning

**Generalization:** Observed pattern in a sample is projected to a larger population.
- "Every swan I've seen is white. Therefore, all swans are white." (This was believed until black swans were discovered in Australia.)

**Analogical reasoning:** Two things share known properties, so they likely share another.
- "This drug worked on mice. Mice are biologically similar to humans. Therefore, it will probably work on humans."

**Inference to the best explanation (abduction):**
- The evidence is X. The best explanation of X is Y. Therefore, Y is probably true.

### Evaluating Inductive Arguments

- **Sample size:** Larger, more diverse samples produce stronger generalizations.
- **Relevance:** Are the shared properties in an analogy actually relevant to the conclusion?
- **Competing explanations:** For abductive reasoning, have alternative explanations been considered?
- **Base rates:** How likely is the conclusion independent of the specific evidence?

## Informal Fallacies

Fallacies are errors in reasoning that can be psychologically persuasive despite being logically invalid.

### Fallacies of Relevance

**Ad Hominem:** Attacking the person making the argument rather than the argument itself.
- "You can't trust his views on climate change — he drives an SUV."
- The character of the speaker does not determine the truth of the claim.

**Appeal to Authority:** Citing an authority figure as evidence when the authority is not relevant or the claim is contested.
- "A famous physicist says consciousness is quantum, so it must be true." (Physics expertise does not transfer to consciousness studies.)

**Appeal to Popularity (Ad Populum):** Claiming something is true because many people believe it.
- "Most people believe in free will, so free will must exist."

**Appeal to Emotion:** Substituting an emotional response for logical support.
- "This policy must be wrong — just think of the children!"

**Red Herring:** Introducing an irrelevant topic to divert attention from the original issue.

### Fallacies of Ambiguity

**Equivocation:** Using a word with multiple meanings as if it meant the same thing throughout.
- "The law says you cannot make false statements. Novels contain false statements. Therefore, novels are illegal." ("False statements" means different things in each premise.)

**Composition:** Assuming what is true of parts is true of the whole.
- "Every player on the team is excellent, so the team must be excellent."

**Division:** Assuming what is true of the whole is true of its parts.
- "The team is the best in the league, so every player must be the best at their position."

### Fallacies of Presumption

**Begging the Question (Petitio Principii):** Assuming the conclusion in the premises.
- "God exists because the Bible says so, and the Bible is true because it is the word of God."

**False Dichotomy:** Presenting only two options when more exist.
- "You're either with us or against us."

**Slippery Slope:** Claiming that one step will inevitably lead to extreme consequences without justification for each step in the chain.
- "If we allow X, next it will be Y, then Z, then total chaos."

**Straw Man:** Misrepresenting an opponent's position to make it easier to attack.
- "Vegetarians think animals are more important than people."

## Using Logic in Philosophical Dialogue

### Argument Reconstruction

When a seeker presents a position, reconstruct the argument:

1. Identify the conclusion: what are they claiming?
2. Identify the premises: what reasons are they offering?
3. Make hidden premises explicit
4. Evaluate: is the argument valid? Are the premises true?

### Charitable Interpretation

Always interpret the seeker's argument in its strongest form before critiquing it. If there are multiple ways to read a claim, choose the most defensible one. This is not about being nice — it is about engaging with the best version of the idea.

### The Socratic Application

When you identify a fallacy or weak argument:
- Do not announce "That's a fallacy." This is often perceived as dismissive.
- Instead, ask a question that exposes the issue: "Would that reasoning also apply to this different case?" or "What if the opposite conclusion were supported by the same type of evidence?"

### When Logic Reaches Its Limits

Logic is a tool, not a worldview. Some philosophical questions cannot be resolved by logic alone:
- Questions of value (what matters) cannot be derived from questions of fact (what is)
- Existential questions about meaning exceed what formal logic can address
- Aesthetic and spiritual experience operate in domains where logical analysis is useful but not sufficient

Acknowledge these limits honestly. The philosopher who only has logic is like a carpenter who only has a hammer.
