# Stoic Practice: Philosophy as a Way of Life

## The Core Doctrine

Stoicism is not about suppressing emotions. It is about understanding what is within your control and directing your energy there, while accepting what is not within your control with equanimity.

### The Dichotomy of Control (Epictetus)

The fundamental Stoic division:

**Within your control (eph' hemin):**
- Your judgments and opinions
- Your desires and aversions
- Your intentions and efforts
- Your responses to events

**Not within your control (ouk eph' hemin):**
- Other people's actions and opinions
- Your body (ultimately — illness, aging, death)
- Your reputation
- External circumstances, outcomes, weather, politics
- The past

**The principle:** Direct all effort toward what is within your control. Release attachment to what is not. This is not passivity — it is strategic clarity about where your power actually lies.

### Application in Dialogue

When a seeker is distressed, help them sort:

> Seeker: "I'm worried my presentation will go badly."
> Philosopher: "Let's separate what you can control from what you can't. You can control: preparation, practice, what you say, how you respond to questions. You cannot control: audience reactions, technical failures, whether people agree with you. Where do you want to put your energy?"

## Daily Stoic Practices

### Morning Premeditatio Malorum (Premeditation of Adversity)

Each morning, before the day begins, contemplate potential difficulties:

- "Today I may encounter ungrateful, arrogant, dishonest, or spiteful people." (Marcus Aurelius, Meditations 2.1)
- "The meeting may not go as planned."
- "Traffic, delays, frustrations are likely."
- "Someone I care about may disappoint me."

**Purpose:** Not pessimism but inoculation. When difficulties arise, they do not catch you unprepared. You have already met them in thought and chosen your response.

### Negative Visualization (Premeditatio Futurorum Malorum)

Regularly contemplate the loss of what you value:

- Imagine losing your health, your home, a loved one
- Imagine your career ending, your savings gone
- Imagine this being your last day

**Purpose:** Two-fold:
1. **Gratitude:** Contemplating loss reveals the value of what you have. You stop taking things for granted.
2. **Resilience:** If the worst happens, you have already practiced facing it mentally. The shock is diminished.

**Important:** This is not anxiety or catastrophizing. It is a brief, deliberate practice done from a place of stability. If it increases anxiety rather than reducing it, step back and work with the dichotomy of control first.

### Evening Review (Seneca's Practice)

Each evening, review the day:

- What did I do well today?
- Where did I fall short of my principles?
- What could I do differently tomorrow?

Seneca's approach is not self-flagellation but honest self-assessment. The tone is that of a teacher reviewing a student's work — firm but fair, noting both progress and areas for improvement.

### The View from Above (Marcus Aurelius)

Mentally zoom out from your current situation:

- See yourself in your room
- See the building, the neighborhood, the city
- See the country, the continent, the planet
- See the planet as a tiny point in the vastness of space
- See the vastness of time — your life as a brief moment in cosmic history

**Purpose:** Perspective. The thing that seems catastrophic right now is small when seen from sufficient distance. This is not dismissal — it is proportion.

## Key Stoic Concepts

### Amor Fati: Love of Fate

Not merely accepting what happens but embracing it — even the difficult parts. The Stoic does not say "I will tolerate this hardship" but "this hardship is mine, and I will use it."

Nietzsche adopted this concept: the test of a life well-lived is whether you would choose to live it again, exactly as it was, including every pain and failure.

**In practice:**
- When something unwanted happens, ask: "How can I use this?"
- Instead of "Why is this happening to me?" try "What is this teaching me?"
- The obstacle is the way — every difficulty contains the material for growth.

### Virtue as the Sole Good

The Stoics identified four cardinal virtues:

**Wisdom (sophia):** The knowledge of what is truly good, bad, and indifferent. The capacity to see clearly.

**Courage (andreia):** Not just physical bravery but moral courage — the willingness to do what is right despite fear, social pressure, or personal cost.

**Justice (dikaiosyne):** Treating others fairly and contributing to the common good. Recognizing your obligations to the human community.

**Temperance (sophrosyne):** Self-discipline and moderation. Not excess, not deprivation — the measured middle.

Everything else — wealth, health, reputation, pleasure — is a "preferred indifferent." It is reasonable to prefer health to illness, but health is not a moral good. Only virtue is.

### Memento Mori: Remember Death

Death is not to be feared but remembered. The awareness of mortality clarifies priorities, dissolves pettiness, and motivates virtuous action.

- "You could leave life right now. Let that determine what you do and say and think." (Marcus Aurelius)
- This is not morbid. It is urgent. If today were your last day, would you spend it worrying about what that person said about you?

## Stoic Responses to Common Situations

### When Insulted or Criticized

Before reacting, ask:
1. Is the criticism true? If so, be grateful — someone has shown you an area for growth.
2. Is it false? Then it does not apply to you, and there is nothing to react to.
3. Is the person trying to hurt you? Their intention is their problem. Your reaction is your choice.

Epictetus: "If someone succeeds in provoking you, realize that your mind is complicit in the provocation."

### When Anxious About the Future

1. Apply the dichotomy of control. Sort controllable from uncontrollable.
2. Take action on what is controllable.
3. Practice premeditatio — imagine the worst outcome. Can you survive it? (Usually yes.)
4. Return attention to the present moment. The future does not exist yet.

### When Suffering Loss

1. Everything you had was on loan. Nothing was permanently yours.
2. Gratitude for the time you had, rather than resentment for the time lost.
3. The pain of loss is the price of having loved or valued something. Would you choose never to have had it?
4. Grief is natural and should be felt — but it should not destroy the capacity to live well.

### When Tempted by Excess

1. Ask: "Is this necessary? Is this contributing to my flourishing or detracting from it?"
2. The test of enough: if you cannot enjoy what you have, more will not help.
3. Practice voluntary discomfort occasionally — cold exposure, simple meals, sleeping on the floor — to remember that comfort is a preference, not a need.

## The Stoic Stance in Dialogue

When applying Stoic philosophy in conversation:

- Focus on what the seeker can actually do, not on what they cannot change
- Be direct. Stoic philosophy does not comfort through reassurance but through clarity.
- Do not dismiss emotions — acknowledge them, then redirect energy toward constructive response
- Use the Stoic tests: "Is this within your control? Is this virtuous? Will this matter in a year?"
- Model tranquility — the Stoic teacher demonstrates equanimity, not just describes it
