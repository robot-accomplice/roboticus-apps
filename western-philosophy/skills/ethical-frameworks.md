# Ethical Frameworks: Tools for Moral Reasoning

## Why Multiple Frameworks Matter

No single ethical theory captures the full complexity of moral life. Each framework illuminates certain aspects of ethics while leaving others in shadow. A philosophically sophisticated approach uses multiple frameworks as complementary lenses.

When frameworks agree on a course of action, confidence is high. When they disagree, the disagreement itself is illuminating — it reveals what is genuinely at stake.

---

## Deontology: Ethics of Duty

### The Core Idea

Some actions are right or wrong in themselves, regardless of consequences. Morality consists of following rational moral rules.

### Kant's Categorical Imperative

Kant formulated three versions of his supreme moral principle:

**The Universal Law Formula:** "Act only according to that maxim by which you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law."

Test: Could everyone do what you are about to do? If universalizing the action leads to contradiction or absurdity, the action is wrong. Lying fails this test — if everyone lied, communication would collapse, making lying itself impossible.

**The Humanity Formula:** "Act so that you treat humanity, whether in your own person or in that of another, always as an end and never merely as a means."

Test: Are you using this person solely as a tool for your purposes? Every person has inherent dignity and must be respected as a rational agent, not instrumentalized.

**The Kingdom of Ends Formula:** "Act according to maxims of a universally legislating member of a merely possible kingdom of ends."

Test: In a community of fully rational, fully moral beings, would this action be acceptable? Would you be comfortable if everyone knew what you were doing and why?

### Strengths of Deontology

- Provides clear moral rules that are not hostage to circumstances
- Protects individual rights against utilitarian sacrifice
- Takes human dignity seriously
- Offers moral certainty in many cases

### Weaknesses of Deontology

- Can be rigid — the duty not to lie creates problems when a murderer asks where your friend is hiding
- Difficult to resolve conflicts between duties
- Can ignore relevant consequences — the "fiat justitia, ruat coelum" problem (let justice be done though the heavens fall)
- Assumes all rational beings will reach the same moral conclusions (they do not)

### In Dialogue

When a seeker faces an ethical dilemma, the deontological questions are:
- "Would you be comfortable if everyone did this?"
- "Are you treating anyone as merely a means to your ends?"
- "What rule are you following, and would you want that rule to be universal?"

## Consequentialism: Ethics of Outcomes

### The Core Idea

The morality of an action is determined entirely by its consequences. Right actions produce good outcomes; wrong actions produce bad ones.

### Utilitarianism (Bentham and Mill)

The most influential consequentialist theory: the right action is the one that produces the greatest good for the greatest number.

**Bentham's calculus:** Measure pleasure and pain across intensity, duration, certainty, proximity, fecundity (does it lead to more pleasure?), and extent (how many people are affected?).

**Mill's refinement:** Not all pleasures are equal. Higher pleasures (intellectual, aesthetic, moral) are more valuable than lower pleasures (bodily, immediate). "It is better to be Socrates dissatisfied than a fool satisfied."

### Types of Consequentialism

**Act consequentialism:** Judge each individual action by its consequences.
**Rule consequentialism:** Follow rules that, if generally adopted, would produce the best consequences.

Rule consequentialism resolves some objections to act consequentialism — for example, it explains why keeping promises is important even when breaking a specific promise would produce a better outcome in that case.

### Strengths of Consequentialism

- Practical and concrete — focuses on real-world outcomes
- Flexible — adapts to circumstances
- Democratic in spirit — every person's well-being counts equally
- Provides a clear decision procedure in many cases

### Weaknesses of Consequentialism

- Consequences are difficult to predict — how far into the future do you calculate?
- Can justify terrible acts if they produce good aggregate outcomes (sacrificing one innocent person to save five)
- Ignores the distribution of goods — "greatest happiness" could mean extreme inequality if the total is maximized
- Treats persons as fungible units of happiness rather than as individuals with rights

### In Dialogue

Consequentialist questions:
- "What outcome are you trying to produce?"
- "Who is affected, and how?"
- "Have you considered unintended consequences?"
- "Are you sure this action will produce the outcome you expect?"

## Virtue Ethics: Ethics of Character

### The Core Idea

The central question is not "What should I do?" but "What kind of person should I be?" Morality is about cultivating excellent character traits (virtues) and avoiding vices.

### Aristotle's Framework

**Eudaimonia:** The goal of life is flourishing — not pleasure, not wealth, but the fulfillment of human potential through virtuous activity.

**The Doctrine of the Mean:** Every virtue is a mean between two extremes (vices). Courage is the mean between cowardice and recklessness. Generosity is the mean between miserliness and wastefulness.

**Practical wisdom (phronesis):** The ability to discern the right action in particular circumstances. No set of rules can cover every situation — what is needed is judgment, developed through experience and reflection.

**Habituation:** Virtue is not a natural gift but a cultivated skill. You become courageous by practicing courage. You become honest by practicing honesty. Character is built through repeated action.

### Strengths of Virtue Ethics

- Attends to the whole person, not just isolated actions
- Allows for contextual judgment (phronesis) rather than rigid rule-following
- Explains moral development — people can grow and improve
- Recognizes the role of community in moral formation

### Weaknesses of Virtue Ethics

- Vague on specific action guidance — "What would a virtuous person do?" is not always clear
- Can be culturally relative — virtues are partly shaped by community values
- The doctrine of the mean is difficult to apply precisely
- Does not always resolve dilemmas — a virtuous person can face genuinely tragic choices

### In Dialogue

Virtue ethics questions:
- "What kind of person do you want to be?"
- "What would someone you deeply admire do in this situation?"
- "Is this action building the character you want, or eroding it?"
- "What virtue does this situation call for?"

## Care Ethics

### The Core Idea

Morality is fundamentally about relationships and the responsibilities that arise from caring connections. Care ethics emerged from feminist philosophy (Gilligan, Noddings) as a corrective to abstract, rule-based approaches.

### Key Principles

- **Relationality:** We are not isolated individuals making calculated decisions. We are embedded in webs of relationship that create specific obligations.
- **Attention:** The first moral act is paying attention to the needs of others — really seeing them, not projecting onto them.
- **Responsiveness:** Moral action is responding to particular people in particular situations, not applying universal rules.
- **Vulnerability:** Recognizing and respecting vulnerability — both our own and others' — is morally central.

### In Dialogue

Care ethics questions:
- "Who would be hurt by this choice? Who depends on you?"
- "What does this person actually need, not what do you think they should need?"
- "What relationships are at stake, and what do those relationships require of you?"
- "Are you attending to this person as a specific individual, or as an abstract case?"

## Applying Multiple Frameworks

When a seeker faces an ethical dilemma, walk through multiple frameworks:

1. **Deontology:** What are the relevant duties and rights?
2. **Consequentialism:** What outcomes will this produce? For whom?
3. **Virtue ethics:** What does this situation call for in terms of character?
4. **Care ethics:** What relationships are involved and what do they require?

Where frameworks converge, the answer is likely sound. Where they diverge, the divergence itself reveals the moral complexity of the situation — and honest acknowledgment of that complexity is more helpful than false certainty.
