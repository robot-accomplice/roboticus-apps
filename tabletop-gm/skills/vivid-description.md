# Vivid Description for GMs

## The Five Senses Framework

Most GMs default to sight. Sight is the weakest sense for creating presence. The other four senses are underused and disproportionately powerful.

| Sense | Question to Ask | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Sight** | What is visible? Light quality, color, movement, scale? | "The chamber is lit by a single oil lamp, throwing long shadows that shift with the draft." |
| **Sound** | What is audible? Distance, direction, rhythm, quality? | "Somewhere behind the wall: water dripping. Irregular. Then silence." |
| **Smell** | What does the air carry? Age, decay, food, fire, earth? | "The smell reaches you before the sight does — wet stone and old ash, and something sharper underneath it." |
| **Touch/Temperature** | Is the air cold, humid, stale? What do surfaces feel like? | "The metal of the door is cold enough to sting. Not normal cold — cold in a way that feels deliberate." |
| **Taste** | What does the environment put in the mouth? | "The air here tastes flat, recycled — whatever breathes in this place has used up something in it." |

**Rule for new scenes:** Anchor with two senses. Sight + one other. More than three in an opening description becomes a list.

**Use unexpected senses for emphasis:** A smell before a threat appears. A sound before a presence is visible. Taste in an environment that shouldn't have one. These signal to players that something is wrong without telling them directly.

## Describing Environments

### Natural Environments

**Forest**
> Day: "Light comes through the canopy in pieces — broken, moving. The ground is soft underfoot, a decade of fallen leaves. Something in the branches is tracking your movement; you can hear it keeping pace."

> Night: "The forest closes entirely after dark. The path is a memory. Your light goes about ten feet before the trees take it. Beyond that: nothing. Not silence — the opposite of silence. Everything out there is awake."

**Cave / Underground**
> "The passage narrows to single file. Your torch makes the walls jump. The stalactites are close enough to brush your shoulder, and water runs somewhere below the floor — you can hear it but not see it. The air is cold, damp, and completely still."

**Ocean / Coast**
> "The harbor at low tide smells of brine, rotting rope, and fish scales. Gulls are working the shallows. The ships creak as they settle, a low wooden groan that sounds like something alive and in pain."

### Urban Environments

**Tavern**
> "The common room is warm, loud, and close. Eight conversations going at once, none of them for your ears. The fire has been burning all day and the smoke has soaked into everything — the wood, the rushes on the floor, the clothes of the man next to you. A barmaid moves through the crowd with the efficiency of someone who has done this ten thousand times."

**Market**
> "The morning market is already in full noise. Vendors calling, animals protesting, the clatter of scales and coin. The smell moves in waves — bread, dung, smoke, spice — depending on which way you face. Everything is for sale, including information, if you know which stalls to approach."

**Castle / Keep**
> "The great hall is built for intimidation. The ceiling is forty feet up and the fireplace at the far end is large enough to stand in. Your footsteps echo. The lord's chair at the high table is elevated just enough that you look up at whoever sits in it. That is not an accident."

### Dungeon Environments

**Corridor**
> "The corridor goes thirty feet before the torchlight fails. Stone floor, stone ceiling, stone walls — but the stone is wrong here. Not natural. Cut, and cut deliberately, by something with different proportions than a human hand."

**Chamber with Trap**
> "The room is larger than the corridor suggested. On the far wall, a door. Between you and it, nothing you can see — but the floor is slightly too clean. No dust, no debris. Whatever uses this room passed through here recently, or something removes what falls."

**Boss Chamber**
> "The passage opens into a vault. The ceiling is lost in darkness above. The walls are covered in something that might be carvings or might be damage — at this distance you can't tell. In the center, a raised dais. On it: what you came for. But the air is wrong. Something in here has been waiting."

## Combat Hit Descriptions by Damage Type

Describe hits in terms of physical effect, not mechanical outcome. Never say "you take 8 damage." Say what 8 damage looks like.

### Slashing
> "The blade catches them across the ribs. The leather splits. Blood dark against the torn material."

> "Your sword finds the gap beneath the pauldron. The cut is clean and deep. They stagger."

### Piercing
> "The bolt punches through the shoulder, momentum spinning them half around before they catch themselves."

> "The spear tip drives in below the collarbone. A wet sound. They stop moving forward."

### Bludgeoning
> "The hammer catches them across the jaw. Their head snaps sideways. They don't fall, but their stance breaks entirely."

> "The club hits the forearm they raised to block. The block fails. They're going to feel that bone tomorrow."

### Fire
> "The flames take them across the chest. The smell of burning cloth, then burning hair. They beat at it with their hands for a moment before they're moving again, clothing still smoking."

> "The fireball catches the edge of the group. One of them is burning. He doesn't scream — the shock hasn't broken yet."

### Cold
> "The frost hits them and they seize up — not frozen, but tight, every muscle contracted. Their breath clouds in front of them. They move like something old."

> "Ice forms in their beard, in their eyelashes. The cold has gotten into the wound. They're fighting on momentum alone."

### Necrotic
> "Where the energy touches them, the flesh goes gray, then dark — not bruised, drained. They look momentarily hollow."

> "The life visibly goes out of that part of them. The muscle doesn't work right. They compensate with the rest of their body, but the arm hangs wrong."

### Radiant
> "The light hits them like a physical weight. Their shadow burns. For a moment you see through them — the outline of something fighting not to come apart."

> "They recoil as if struck by something they fundamentally cannot absorb. The light leaves a mark that doesn't look like a burn — cleaner, simpler, more final."

### Lightning
> "The bolt travels up through them from ground to shoulder. Every muscle fires at once, then releases. They're standing on reflex, not intent."

> "The discharge leaves scorch marks on the stone behind them and the smell of ozone hangs in the chamber like an accusation."

### Thunder
> "The concussion hits them like a closed door swung hard — fast, flat, total. The sound stops being a sound and becomes a force."

> "The wave knocks two of them back three steps. One of them is shaking their head, trying to clear it. Their ears are ringing. You can tell from the way they're looking at you."

### Psychic
> "Their expression changes first — surprise, then pain from behind the eyes. Whatever reached them reached inside."

> "They don't look hurt in any physical sense. They look like someone who just remembered something terrible."

### Poison
> "The color changes in their face fast. Sweat. Then a tremor in the hands. Their movements lose precision. They're still fighting but the poison is already winning."

## Describing Magic: Visual Effects by Spell School

### Evocation (dramatic, kinetic, elemental)
> "The air in front of the wizard compresses for a fraction of a second, then releases. The fireball expands from nothing — not thrown, summoned — and by the time you understand what you're seeing, it's already there."

Sound: sharp, percussive, immediate. Something is happening to the world.

### Illusion (subtle, perceptual, wrong in small ways)
> "The duplicate appears gradually. Not as a sudden event — as a slow insistence that it was always there and you simply hadn't noticed."

The tell of illusion magic, described well: things that are almost right. A shadow that moves slightly before the figure does. A voice that echoes from the wrong direction.

### Necromancy (unsettling, against nature, cold)
> "The magic moves wrong. Not through the air but through the absence in the air — the part of the room where warmth doesn't reach. The corpse doesn't stand up so much as the idea of lying down leaves it."

### Conjuration (portal-like, spatial, displacement)
> "Reality makes room for the creature rather than the creature entering reality. A doorway that isn't a door. A presence that was somewhere else, then wasn't."

### Divination (still, interior, revelatory)
> "Nothing visible changes. The wizard closes his eyes and opens them. What he sees when he opens them is not what you see. His expression is the only evidence anything happened."

### Abjuration (defensive, structural, layered)
> "The ward doesn't announce itself. The bolt hits it and stops. Not deflected — absorbed. The ward holds it for a moment, processes it, and releases nothing."

### Transmutation (organic, physical change, gradual or sudden)
> "The stone flows like clay under the warmth of the spell, finding a new shape without fracturing. When the warmth leaves it, it is stone again — and has always been stone in this new shape."

### Enchantment (social, internal, invisible)
> "You didn't see anything change. You won't see anything change. But when she spoke, something in the room shifted — not the air, not the light. The attention."

## Describing NPCs

Give each significant NPC one specific physical detail and one behavioral tell. More is forgettable. One detail, done well, is unforgettable.

**Appearance:** Choose the one thing a stranger would notice first. Not a complete portrait — a feature.
> "He's taller than expected, and his hands are the hands of a laborer despite the robes."

> "She doesn't blink at the normal rate. Slightly too infrequent. It makes every interaction feel like being studied."

**Voice:** Pitch, pace, accent, formality. Use it consistently.
> "He speaks slowly, chooses every word. Not because he lacks them — because he doesn't trust the listener to hear correctly."

**Mannerism:** One physical tic. Make it legible when he's lying, nervous, or confident.
> "She touches the ring on her left hand when she changes the subject."

## Weather and Atmosphere as Mood Tools

Weather is not background. It is character. Use it to establish tone before a word of dialogue.

| Weather | Mood Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Heavy rain | Pressure, urgency, isolation | "The city is gray and closed. Everyone is hurrying. No one wants to be seen." |
| Fog | Mystery, concealment, dread | "The road vanishes ahead. Sound carries strangely. You hear the river before you see the bridge." |
| Clear, bright | False security, exposure, clarity | "The morning is beautiful. The bodies look wrong against all that light." |
| Heat | Tension, fatigue, irritability | "Three days without shade. The party has stopped talking. There's nothing to say that won't sound like an argument." |
| First snow | Stillness, change, endings | "The snow makes everything equally white and quiet. The world is simpler than it was this morning." |
| Storm (lightning) | Chaos, revelation, drama | "The lightning turns the castle white for a fraction of a second, long enough to see the figure in the window." |

## Show, Don't Tell — Describe Effects, Not Mechanics

Never say: "The NPC is lying."
Instead: "He answers your question immediately, without pause. He doesn't look at you when he does."

Never say: "The room is dangerous."
Instead: "There are three exits. The floor near the center is discolored in a rough circle. Old oil stain, maybe — or not."

Never say: "The enemy is afraid."
Instead: "He's backing up. Not retreating in the tactical sense — backing up. His weapon is still out but he's not using it."

The player's job is to interpret. The GM's job is to provide accurate sensory data. Trust players to draw the right conclusions. When they don't, that's useful too — it tells you something about how they're reading the world.

## Scaling Description Intensity

Not every moment gets the same treatment. Match description length and density to the emotional weight of the moment.

### Casual Exploration (brief)
One or two details. Keep moving.
> "The hallway continues north. There are doors on both sides — closed. The torches are well-maintained. Someone tends this place."

### Investigation or Social Scene (moderate)
Three to five details. Build the picture fully. Give players material to engage with.
> "The study is lived-in. Books not just shelved but used — open, dog-eared, annotated in the margins. A cold cup of tea on the desk beside an inkwell that hasn't dried. Someone was here recently and left quickly. On the wall behind the desk: a map. But you need to get closer to read it."

### Tense or Climactic Moment (detailed)
Full sensory engagement. Slow time down. Let the players feel the weight.
> "The ritual circle is active. The symbols on the floor are burning with cold blue light — not fire, not anything fire does. The air has a sound to it that isn't a sound: a pressure behind the ears, in the teeth. At the center of the circle, the figure is changing. You know what it was. You don't have a word for what it's becoming."

### Combat Round (punchy and fast)
Short, kinetic, immediate. Combat description should accelerate, not interrupt.
> "He's fast. Faster than he looked. The blade comes from the left — lower than expected."
> "You hit him. He goes back a step. He's grinning."
> "The first two bolts miss. The third doesn't."
