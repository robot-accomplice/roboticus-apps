# GM Guide

## Adventure Design

### The Three-Act Structure

Every good session follows a natural arc. Plan for it; let the players break it if they want to.

**Act 1 — The Hook (15–20% of session time)**
- Present a clear call to action: a threat, a mystery, a job, a desperate plea.
- Establish stakes immediately. Who suffers if the party does nothing?
- Give the players at least two paths forward — never only one.

**Act 2 — The Journey (60–70% of session time)**
- Complications arise. The obvious path costs something unexpected.
- Introduce subplots: a moral dilemma, a new NPC with an agenda, a discovery that recontextualizes the hook.
- Build toward a point of no return — a moment where the party's choices have locked in an outcome.

**Act 3 — The Climax and Fallout (15–25% of session time)**
- The confrontation: a boss fight, a negotiation under pressure, a race against time.
- Resolution: what changed? Who was lost? What did the party earn?
- The hook for next time: plant it in the last 5 minutes — never let the session end without a question the players want answered.

### Pillars of Adventure

Balance these three across every session:

| Pillar | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Exploration** | Navigating wilderness, mapping dungeons, discovering history |
| **Social** | Negotiating with factions, gaining NPC trust, making deals |
| **Combat** | Encounters with hostile creatures, chases, ambushes |

A session that is 100% combat will exhaust players. A session with no combat may feel low-stakes.
Aim for at least two pillars per session.

## Session Pacing

**The 90-Minute Rule:** If a scene has been running for 90 minutes without a meaningful decision or
revelation, cut it or accelerate. Prolonged scenes feel like mud.

**The Clock:** Give the party a time pressure that ticks visibly. The ship leaves at dawn. The
prisoner is executed at noon. The ritual completes at the next full moon. A ticking clock focuses
the session without railroading.

**Cutting Scenes:** Learn to cut to the action. "You spend two hours traveling — describe one thing
you talk about on the road, then you arrive." Do not narrate every step.

**Scene Transitions:** Always end a scene on momentum. A revelation, a threat, a choice that must
be made. Then cut. Start the next scene already in motion.

## Improvisation Toolkit

### "Yes, And"

When a player does something unexpected that would work logically, say yes and add a complication:
- "Yes, you pick the lock — and you hear voices on the other side of the door."
- "Yes, your intimidation works — and now the captain's lieutenant is eyeing you with murder in his expression."

### "Yes, But"

When yes is right but a cost is appropriate:
- "Yes, the fire spreads to the tapestry — but now the guards have noticed the smoke."
- "Yes, you catch him before he falls — but you're now hanging over the edge with him."

### "No, But"

Reserve hard no for when the action literally cannot succeed. Soften it with an alternative:
- "The door is sealed by magic your knock can't break — but there's a servant's entrance around the side."
- "The merchant won't sell to you today — but his apprentice is watching you with obvious interest."

### Improv NPC Names (use dice tool for randomization)

**Consonant prefixes:** Bran, Sev, Kal, Mir, Tar, Vel, Dorn, Hal, Lyss, Aer
**Common suffixes:** -an, -ik, -ys, -or, -eth, -wyn, -ald, -ara, -en, -ull

Combine one prefix and one suffix for an instant name. Add a short descriptor: "Sevik the Pale",
"Miror of the Docklands", "Tareth who Laughs Last."

## Random Tables

Use the dice tool to generate results from these tables during play.

### Scene Complication (d10)

| d10 | Complication |
|---|---|
| 1 | An unexpected third party arrives with competing interests |
| 2 | Something valuable breaks, is stolen, or is lost |
| 3 | One of the party's allies turns out to be compromised |
| 4 | The enemy has already left — the trail is cold |
| 5 | Help arrives — but it comes with a cost |
| 6 | The environment becomes dangerous (fire, flood, collapse) |
| 7 | A secret about one of the NPCs is revealed at the worst moment |
| 8 | The party's actions have already had unintended consequences elsewhere |
| 9 | An old debt or relationship resurfaces |
| 10 | What seemed like one problem is actually two |

### NPC Motivation (d8)

| d8 | Motivation |
|---|---|
| 1 | Survival (theirs or someone they love) |
| 2 | Revenge for a past wrong |
| 3 | Ambition — they want power, status, or wealth |
| 4 | Loyalty to a person, cause, or code |
| 5 | Fear of what happens if they do nothing |
| 6 | Grief — they are trying to undo or honor a loss |
| 7 | Curiosity — they want to know the truth |
| 8 | Duty — obligation, whether they chose it or not |

### Dungeon Room Contents (d6)

| d6 | Contents |
|---|---|
| 1 | Empty — but with evidence of recent occupation |

| 5 | Treasure (guarded or hidden) |
| 6 | Information — a clue, a map, a prisoner who knows something |

## Treasure and Rewards

### Individual Treasure (per creature defeated, CR-scaled)

| CR | Coins | Items |
|---|---|---|
| 0–4 | 2d6 sp per creature | Common item (50% chance) |
| 5–10 | 4d6 gp per creature | Uncommon item (25% chance) |
| 11–16 | 2d6 × 10 gp | Rare item (15% chance) |
| 17+ | 4d6 × 100 gp | Very rare or legendary item (10% chance) |

### Hoard Treasure (per dungeon or significant location)

Scale to the CR of the encounter that guards it. A CR 5 hoard might contain:
- 400–1000 gp in coin, gems, or trade goods
- 1d4 spell scrolls (levels 1–3)
- One uncommon magic item

### Meaningful Rewards

Coin is forgettable. Make rewards memorable:
- **Named weapons** with a history
- **Favors and relationships** — the lord owes you, the guild marks you as friends
- **Information** — the map, the name, the secret that changes everything
- **Property** — a deed to a building, a ship, a keep in disrepair

## Milestone Leveling

Milestone leveling ties advancement to story achievements rather than accumulated XP.
It keeps all characters at the same level and makes leveling feel earned.

**Suggested milestone triggers:**

| Level | Achievement |
|---|---|
| 2 | Complete the opening adventure; survive the inciting crisis |
| 3 | Establish a clear goal and commit to the central conflict |
| 4 | Overcome the first major obstacle; deal with a significant antagonist or faction |
| 5 | Reach the midpoint; a key revelation reframes the stakes |
| 6 | Survive a major setback; the situation has fundamentally changed |
| 7–9 | Progress through the campaign's core arc (3 milestones, GM-designed) |
| 10+ | Reserved for campaigns that continue past the standard arc |

Announce milestones at the end of a session, not mid-scene. Let the players level up between sessions if possible.

## Social Encounters

Social encounters have stakes, objectives, and resolution just like combat.

### Structure

1. **Establish the NPC's starting attitude:** Hostile / Unfriendly / Indifferent / Friendly / Helpful
2. **Identify what the party needs** from this NPC.
3. **Set the DC** for the relevant social skill: CHA (Persuasion, Deception, Intimidation, Performance).
4. **Run the conversation** — rolls support the roleplay, they do not replace it.

### Attitude Shifts

A successful skill check shifts the NPC's attitude by one step. Three successes (not necessarily consecutive) move from Indifferent to Helpful.

Each failed check risks worsening the attitude by one step or closing off an approach entirely.

**Critical fails on social checks:** The NPC now actively works against the party's goal.
**Critical success:** The NPC is moved beyond what a normal success would achieve — they offer a secret, a favor, or go out of their way unprompted.

### Social DC Guidelines

| Situation | DC |
|---|---|
| Asking for reasonable help from someone neutral | 10 |
| Convincing someone to act against minor self-interest | 15 |
| Getting an unfriendly NPC to cooperate | 20 |
| Persuading someone to act against their values or significant self-interest | 25 |
| Moving a hostile NPC to neutrality in one exchange | 30 (nearly impossible) |

## Traps and Puzzles

### Traps

Every trap has three elements:
1. **The trigger** — what sets it off (pressure plate, tripwire, opened container, spoken word)
2. **The effect** — what happens (damage, restraint, alarm, teleportation)
3. **The tell** — the detail that a careful observer could notice (DC 15 Perception or Investigation)

Always give a tell. Players who are actively searching should have a realistic chance to spot it.
Invisible, untriggered traps with no tell are a design failure.

**Disarming:** DC 15 Thieves' Tools check for mechanical traps. Magic traps may require Arcana, a specific spell, or creative lateral thinking.

### Puzzles

Good puzzles:
- Have a logical solution that follows from observable clues
- Can be approached multiple ways (brute force, magic, lateral thinking)
- Reward creative player thinking over specific skill checks
- Have a cost for failure (time, noise, damage) rather than a complete stop

Bad puzzles:
- Require knowledge the players don't have and couldn't reasonably acquire
- Have only one correct solution with no alternatives
- Block critical story progress permanently on failure

When players are stuck: after 15 minutes of real-time effort, have an NPC, a returning player
character, or a lucky investigation check provide a nudge — not the answer, a direction.

## Running a One-Shot

A one-shot is a complete story in one session (typically 3–4 hours).

### Design Principles

- **Pre-generated characters** are your friend. Give each one a personal stake in the hook.
- **Tight scope:** one location, one antagonist, one central question.
- **Accelerated stakes:** the threat is immediate and personal. No time for meandering setup.
- **Built-in character moments:** give each pre-gen at least one scene where their background matters.

### One-Shot Template

| Phase | Time | Content |
|---|---|---|
| Opening | 20 min | The hook lands. Characters meet (if needed). First decision point. |
| Rising Action | 60 min | Two to three encounters (mix of pillars). Complications mount. |
| Mid-Point Revelation | 10 min | Something changes. The antagonist's true plan is partially revealed. |
| Climax | 45 min | Final encounter. Stakes at maximum. Resources depleted. |
| Resolution | 15 min | What changed? Debrief in character. Let players narrate their aftermath. |

### One-Shot Rescue

If the session is running short: add a complication from the Scene Complication table.
If the session is running long: cut a planned encounter and jump to the climax. Say:
"As you prepare to search the lower levels, a sound from above tells you the boss already knows you're here."
