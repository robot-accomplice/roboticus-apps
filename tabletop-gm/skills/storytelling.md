# Storytelling Techniques for GMs

## The Improv Framework: Yes-And / Yes-But / No-But

The three-response model gives GMs a tool for keeping the story alive regardless of what players attempt.

### Yes, And...
Accept the player's premise and **amplify** it.

> Player: "I want to bribe the city guard captain."
> GM: "Yes — and he takes the bribe, but now he considers himself your business partner and starts sending you 'opportunities.'"

Use when: the action is reasonable, fun, and opens new story space.

### Yes, But...
Accept the premise and introduce a **complication or cost**.

> Player: "I want to jump across the rooftop gap."
> GM: "Yes — but you make it, dropping your lantern, which shatters on the cobblestones below. You're across, but now you're in the dark and someone below heard the crash."

Use when: success should come with a price, or the action would otherwise resolve a scene too cleanly.

### No, But...
Deny the literal action but offer an **alternative path**.

> Player: "I try to pick the lock on the vault."
> GM: "No — it's a magical seal, not a mechanical lock, so your picks won't find purchase. But you notice a seam in the stone beside it, as if the whole panel swings outward when triggered some other way."

Use when: outright success is impossible, but "you fail, next" is a dead end.

**What to avoid:** "No, and..." — refusing the action AND closing off alternatives. This generates player frustration without narrative payoff.

## Scene-Setting with Sensory Details

Don't just describe what players see. Activate all five senses.

| Sense | Example |
|---|---|
| **Sight** | "The torchlight catches the edges of freshly disturbed cobblestones." |
| **Sound** | "Somewhere below the floorboards, water is dripping in a slow, irregular rhythm." |
| **Smell** | "The room smells of old incense and something underneath it — iron, faintly sweet." |
| **Touch/Temperature** | "The air at the threshold is noticeably colder than the corridor behind you." |
| **Taste** | "The stew is hot, thick, oversalted — the kind of meal someone made under pressure." |

**Rule of two:** Anchor each new scene with exactly two sensory details. More becomes a monologue. One is thin. Two is immersive.

Save the third and fourth senses for moments of tension or revelation — the smell of smoke when the players haven't seen fire yet is more alarming than announcing "the building is on fire."

## Pacing: Compress and Expand

Pacing is the GM's most powerful tool. Real sessions have unequal rhythm — and that's good.

### Compress time when:
- Travel between story-relevant locations is routine
- A long rest occurs without threat
- Players are handling logistics (buying supplies, scribing spells, training)
- The narrative needs to jump to a more interesting moment

> "The three-day ride north is uneventful. You arrive at the mountain pass as the sun dips below the tree line."

### Slow down (expand time) when:
- Combat begins — switch to initiative, round-by-round
- A critical conversation or negotiation is happening
- A mystery or trap is being investigated
- A player is having a character moment

### The scene-cut technique:
End a scene the moment its purpose is achieved. Don't follow players to the inn, watch them eat, and track them to bed. Cut to: "Morning. What are you doing first?"

## Dramatic Tension

### Raising Stakes
Before a high-stakes roll or encounter, make clear what's on the line. Players care more when they know the cost.

- Name the threat specifically: not "you might fail" but "if the poison isn't treated in the next hour, Thera dies."
- Give players information — tension requires understanding the stakes, not ignorance of them.

### Foreshadowing
Plant a detail two to three scenes before it pays off. When it resolves, players feel clever for having noticed.

- A merchant mentions the river road is unusually quiet. Two sessions later, the players discover why.
- A minor NPC appears nervous whenever the name "Seravyn" comes up. Plant it twice before it becomes a plot point.
- A description includes an unusual architectural detail. It becomes the secret passage.

Keep a short list of planted details so you don't forget to pay them off.

### Callbacks
Reference earlier player choices and story events. This makes the world feel reactive and alive.

> "The blacksmith you helped in Thornfield — his nephew is the soldier standing between you and the gate. He recognizes you."

## The Three Pillars of Play

SRD 5.1 structures play around three activity types. Healthy sessions usually include all three.

### Exploration
Moving through the world, discovering new locations, gathering information, solving environmental challenges.

- Works best when the world has texture: weather, history, factions with visible presence.
- Rewards curiosity: players who poke at things should find things.
- Avoid empty corridors — every room should answer a question or raise one.

### Social Interaction
Talking with NPCs — negotiating, persuading, deceiving, learning.

- Give NPCs wants, not just functions. A merchant who wants to protect his family reacts differently than one who just wants gold.
- Players should be able to achieve through roleplay what they could achieve through rolls — and vice versa.
- Skill checks support roleplay; they don't replace it. A Persuasion roll amplifies a good argument; it doesn't substitute for one.

### Combat
Structured, tactical conflict using initiative and action economy.

- Not every conflict needs to be fought to the death. Enemies can retreat, surrender, or negotiate.
- Vary encounter types: ambush, defense, extraction, escort, chase.
- Ensure every character has at least one meaningful moment per fight.

## Handling Player Derailment

Players will do unexpected things. This is the game. Treat it as a feature, not a malfunction.

**The redirect:** When players go somewhere unexpected, ask yourself: "What would logically be here?" Then populate it. You don't need to have planned it.

**The consequence:** Player actions should change the world. If they ignore the quest to liberate the city's grain stores, the famine happens. Show consequences without punishing players for having their own agenda.

**The yes-but pivot:** When players derail to pursue something that interests them, let them — and weave the main plot back in through that new thread.

**The honest pause:** If a player asks about something you genuinely haven't planned, it's fine to say "You don't know yet" or take a moment. Better a brief pause than an inconsistent improvisation you'll regret.

**The railroad is the failure mode:** Players can always tell when they're being steered. If the story only works one way, the story needs to be more flexible.

## Ending Sessions on Cliffhangers

A strong session ending is worth planning for. Types of effective cliffhangers:

- **The revelation:** The players learn something that reframes what they know. "The body in the crypt — it's wearing the Regent's signet ring."
- **The arrival:** A significant threat, ally, or unknown force appears just as the session ends.
- **The decision:** Players are forced to choose between two bad options, and the session ends before the choice resolves.
- **The cost:** The session's success comes with an unexpected price the players won't know the full weight of yet.

Avoid ending mid-combat or mid-negotiation unless genuinely unavoidable — players will spend the week dreading the outcome, which works, but can also feel unfair if the resolution is then anticlimactic.

## Narrative Consequences for Player Choices

Actions should matter beyond the immediate scene. Track outcomes and surface them later.

| Player Action | Potential Later Consequence |
|---|---|
| Spared the bandit captain | He becomes an informant, or he rebuilds his crew and returns |
| Burned down the manor | The noble family's allies seek revenge; a child was inside |
| Lied to the city watch | A patrol now actively follows the party |
| Gave gold to the temple | The priests remember them; a favor is available |
| Publicly humiliated a merchant | He spreads rumors; prices are worse in this district |

The world is a web of people with memories. That's what makes it feel real.

## Describing Failure as Interesting, Not Punishing

Failed rolls should advance the story, not stop it.

**Bad failure:** "You fail the lock. Nothing happens."
**Good failure:** "The pick snaps in the mechanism. The lock is now jammed — and you heard footsteps upstairs start moving toward the stairs."

**Bad failure:** "You fail the Persuasion check. He doesn't help you."
**Good failure:** "He's not convinced — worse, he's suspicious now. He'll be watching for you. But his reaction tells you something: he knows exactly what you're asking about and is afraid of it."

Failure reveals information, creates complications, raises stakes, or opens new paths. It never just stops the clock.

The only exception: repeated failure at the same action with no new approach should eventually produce a hard "no" — with a clear alternative visible.
