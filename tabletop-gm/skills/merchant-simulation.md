# Merchant Simulation

## Merchant Personality Types

Give each significant merchant one dominant personality trait. It shapes every interaction.

### The Haggler
Sees every transaction as a game to win. Opens with inflated prices. Expects negotiation. Feels cheated if players pay the asking price — a quick sale is a bad sale.

> "Fifty gold? For that blade? My friend, that blade was forged by a smith who trained under the Ironmasters of the mountain. Let's say... one twenty. But I like your face. One hundred. Final offer."

Mechanic: Mark up 25–50% above SRD price. Accept 10–20% below if players succeed on a Persuasion check.

### The Generous Merchant
Fair dealing, honest prices. Believes in long-term relationships over short-term profit. Gives regulars small discounts without being asked.

> "That's the standard price. You helped clear the road last month, so — take two healing potions for the price of one. You earned it."

Mechanic: Prices at SRD baseline. 10% discount for established relationship or notable service.

### The Suspicious Merchant
Assumes everyone is trying to con them. Won't deal with strangers without references. Scrutinizes goods offered for sale intensely.

> "Where did you get this exactly? Because I had a silver candlestick like this stolen from a customer last month, and I'm not going to be the one who fences it."

Mechanic: Requires a DC 13 Persuasion check or a vouching NPC before they'll buy unusual items. Sells without issue once trust is established.

### The Specialist
Deep expertise in one category. Will not sell outside it. Pays above market for rare specimens in their field. Terrible at everything else.

> "Swords? No. Maps? No. Ancient coins from the pre-Imperial period? Now that I'm interested in."

Mechanic: 10–15% above market for relevant items; dismissive or confused about everything else.

### The Guild Representative
Operates within a system. Has fixed prices, limited inventory, and rules they didn't make and can't break. Sympathetic but constrained.

> "I'd love to help you, but the Guild sets the prices. I can't go below the mark. If you want a variance, you'd need to petition the steward — but that takes two weeks."

Mechanic: No haggling on standard goods. Special items may be negotiable with the right NPC contact.

## Haggling Mechanics

Haggling is a short social contest, not a single roll.

### Procedure

1. **Establish the asking price.** The merchant names their price — adjust based on personality type.
2. **Player makes their argument.** Roleplay matters: a compelling reason (bulk purchase, referral, prior relationship, information offered) gives advantage on the check.
3. **Persuasion check vs. merchant's Insight.** Use a contested roll (Persuasion vs. Insight) or a fixed DC based on merchant type:
   - Friendly/generous: DC 10
   - Neutral: DC 13
   - Suspicious/haggler: DC 16
   - Guild/fixed price: DC 20 (or impossible)
4. **Determine outcome.**
   - **Success by 5+:** 20% discount
   - **Success:** 10% discount
   - **Failure:** No discount; merchant becomes mildly annoyed
   - **Failure by 5+:** Price goes up 10%; merchant is offended. Further haggling at disadvantage.

### What actually moves merchants
- Bulk orders (buying 10+ units of one item)
- Cash in advance on commission work
- Offering useful information or a service in return
- Shared faction affiliation or reputation
- A genuine personal connection built over sessions

### What doesn't work
- Threats (unless the party wants enemies)
- Claiming false scarcity ("I heard someone else offers it cheaper")
- Magical compulsion — most merchants have seen it and some have charms against it. It also creates lasting enemies if discovered.

## Supply, Demand, and Location

Prices are not uniform. A village blacksmith and a capital city armorer exist in different economies.

### Village or Remote Settlement
- **Availability:** Basic tools, simple weapons, food, rope, common trade goods. No exotic items.
- **Price adjustment:** +25% on manufactured goods (the smith has fewer suppliers). Food and raw materials may be cheaper.
- **Special:** The local craftsperson may have unique items — a blade made from unusual local iron, a tanner with distinctive leather work.

### Town (1000–5000 people)
- **Availability:** Full range of common equipment. Basic healing potions. Spell components for common spells.
- **Price adjustment:** Near SRD baseline.

### City (10,000+ people)
- **Availability:** Everything in the SRD equipment list. Uncommon magic items from licensed dealers. Specialists for unusual commissions.
- **Price adjustment:** Competition keeps prices at or slightly below baseline. Premium shops charge more for quality, selection, and discretion.

### Major Trade Hub
- **Availability:** Nearly anything can be found or ordered. Including things that shouldn't be for sale.
- **Price adjustment:** Prices vary wildly by quarter — the merchant district is expensive, the docks are cheap, the black market has its own logic.

## Black Market Goods and Illegal Trade

Not everything is sold openly. Illegal goods are available in most cities if players know where to look.

**Finding a fence or black market contact:** Requires a DC 15 Investigation or Persuasion check, or a contact with the Thieves' Guild or equivalent faction. Takes 1d4 hours in a city, 1d4 days in a town.

**Black market pricing:**
- Stolen goods: 25–50% of market value (buyers assume legal risk)
- Regulated items (alchemist's fire, certain poisons, unlicensed weapons): 150–300% of baseline
- Contraband (forbidden magic items, restricted spell components, outlawed materials): negotiated, often with strings attached

**Common black market inventory:**
- Poisons (basic poison: 100 gp baseline; contact poison: 250+ gp)
- Unlicensed thieves' tools and lock picks
- Forgery kits and false papers
- Banned magical texts
- Weapons confiscated from defeated enemies
- Slaves (if your setting includes this — handle with session zero discussion)

**Risk:** Black market sellers may be informants. Purchasing illegal goods can create ongoing obligations, blackmail vulnerability, or direct legal jeopardy.

## Commissioning Custom Items

Players may want items made to specification. Use this framework.

### Time
- Simple item (plain weapon, basic armor): 5–10 days
- Complex item (masterwork quality, unusual materials): 10–30 days
- Exceptional craft (magic weapon focus, exotic materials): 30–90 days

### Cost
- **Labor:** 1 gp/day for journeyman craftsperson; 5 gp/day for master
- **Materials:** 50–100% of base item cost added on top
- **Rush fees:** Double cost for half the time (if the craftsperson agrees)

### Deposit and Completion
- Most craftspeople require 50% upfront, remainder on delivery
- If players supply rare materials (dragonhide, mithral ore, giant's teeth), cost may be reduced significantly — and the craftsperson may have questions about provenance

### Quality tiers
- **Standard:** Baseline stats, no bonus
- **Fine:** Well-balanced or durable; worth 2–3× base price; no mechanical bonus but advantage on checks to appraise it, and it impresses people who notice it
- **Masterwork:** Requires a named master craftsperson; provides a clear narrative hook for a +1 bonus or special property if the GM permits

## Running a Magic Item Shop

Magic items should feel special. A shop full of them on shelves cheapens the wonder.

### How to frame a magic item shop
- The shop deals in **known and catalogued items** — the proprietor has notes on provenance, limitations, quirks. They are not selling mystery boxes.
- Inventory is **limited and specific.** The proprietor has two or three items at any given time, not a full menu.
- **Price is negotiable but significant.** Even a modest Uncommon item at 300 gp is meaningful at low levels.
- The proprietor **evaluates buyers** — they won't sell to someone they believe will misuse an item, because a misused item damages their reputation.

### Sample magic shop inventory (starter city)
- Two potions of healing (50 gp each)
- A Cloak of Protection — listed at 600 gp; attunement required (requires attunement)
- A Bag of Holding — listed at 400 gp; no attunement
- A Sending Stone (one of a matched pair — the other is in the proprietor's possession, so any message reaches them too) — 500 gp

### Magic item shops as story tools
- The proprietor knows where items have been and who's been asking for them
- A rare item "just came in" can be a plot hook for who sold it and why
- The proprietor may trade services for items — "find out who stole my pair of Sending Stones and I'll give you the Bag of Holding at cost"

## Merchant as Quest Giver and Information Source

A good merchant is never just a transaction. Every merchant has:
- **Something they need** (protection, information, a delivery, a recovered debt)
- **Something they know** (they hear everything that passes through their shop)
- **Someone they fear** (a competitor, a creditor, a powerful customer)

Merchants make excellent recurring NPCs because every adventuring party needs to buy things. Use the transaction to move information, introduce hooks, and develop the world.

> "I'll give you the rope at cost — but while I have you, have you seen anything unusual near the Nag's Head Inn? Three of my deliverymen haven't come back from that route."
