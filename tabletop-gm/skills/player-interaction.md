# Player Interaction and Table Management

## Reading the Room

Pay attention to signals that players are engaged, bored, or uncomfortable. Catching these early prevents problems.

### Signs of Engagement
- Players lean forward, make eye contact during scenes
- Players speak in character without prompting
- Players take notes or reference their notes
- Players ask questions about the world beyond their immediate task
- Side conversations stay on-topic (tactics, plans, world questions)

### Signs of Boredom
- Players check phones or drift off during others' scenes
- Short, functional responses ("I attack." "I follow.")
- Players seem to be waiting for their turn rather than participating
- The session has been in one mode too long (all combat, or all NPC conversation with no agency)

**Response:** Change pace. Cut to action, introduce a surprise, ask a player a direct question about their character's state of mind, or call a short break.

### Signs of Discomfort
- Player goes quiet during a specific scene type that was previously fine
- Body language closes off (arms crossed, leaning back)
- A player finds an excuse to leave the room
- A player makes a joke to deflect from a difficult scene
- Someone directly changes the subject

**Response:** Do not push through. Use the X-card or a natural scene break. Check in privately if possible.

## Spotlight Management

Every player should have a meaningful moment in each session. This requires active tracking.

### The Spotlight Problem
Some players are naturally vocal; others are not. A GM who follows energy will consistently spotlight the same two or three players while others fade.

### Techniques

**The direct invitation:** "Korrath, you've been watching this whole conversation. What's your read on the situation?"

**The setup:** Design at least one moment per session that plays to each character's specific abilities or backstory. A rogue who grew up on the docks should have a moment where that matters.

**The redirect:** When one player dominates, find a natural break and turn to another: "While Thessaly is negotiating, what is Mira doing?"

**The follow-up:** After a quiet player acts, follow their action with a consequence that demands another response from them. Don't let their moment vanish without landing.

### Tracking spotlight
Mentally note who has had a meaningful moment (not just a die roll, but an actual decision, revelation, or character beat). If someone hasn't by the second hour, find a way to create that moment.

## Handling Player Conflict

Separate in-character conflict from out-of-character conflict. They require different responses.

### In-Character Conflict (Character vs. Character)
Characters can disagree, compete, have different values. This is healthy and interesting.

- Allow it to play out within the fiction
- Ensure it doesn't paralyze the group for more than one scene
- If it escalates repeatedly, look for a fictional resolution that doesn't require one player to "lose" (both characters are partly right; circumstances force a third option)
- Remind players that their character's goals and the player's desire to have fun are separate

### Out-of-Character Conflict (Player vs. Player)
When real frustration, resentment, or hurt feelings appear between players, stop the game.

- Take a break immediately
- Address it privately if possible — not at the table in front of everyone
- Use the session zero agreements as a reference point
- The GM is not a therapist. You can facilitate, but sustained interpersonal conflict between players needs addressing outside the game, or the table will break down

**Signs OOC conflict is bleeding in:** Players arguing about rules in a way that feels personal; one player consistently targeting another character; a player using in-game fiction to say things to another player they wouldn't say directly.

## Safety Tools

Safety tools create a shared framework for stopping, redirecting, or discussing uncomfortable content before it becomes a problem.

### The X-Card
A physical card (or verbal equivalent) on the table. Any player — including the GM — can tap it at any time to signal "I need this content to stop." No explanation required. No discussion at the table. The scene redirects.

How to introduce it: "If something in the game is making you uncomfortable and you need it to stop, tap the card or say 'X-card.' We just move on, no questions, no pressure."

After the session, the GM can privately check in to understand what happened — but not in the moment.

### Lines and Veils
Established at session zero.

**Lines** are content that will never appear in the game. Hard stops. Not discussed, not hinted at, not "fade to black." Completely absent.

**Veils** are content that may exist in the fiction but is not described explicitly or played out in detail. A veil says "this happened off-screen."

**How to gather them:** Ask each player privately or use an anonymous survey before session zero. Present the compiled list to the group. Add your own as the GM. Write them down and keep them accessible.

### Open Door Policy
Every player can leave any scene, or the session entirely, at any time without explanation. The table doesn't stop, pause, or make a production of it. They come back when ready.

State this explicitly: "If you need to step out, step out. The game continues. You come back when you're ready."

## Session Zero Checklist

Session zero is not optional for a new group or new campaign. It prevents more problems than any other tool.

### Tone and Genre
- What kind of story are we telling? (Heroic epic, gritty survival, political intrigue, comedic, horror?)
- What's the overall power fantasy? (Competent professionals, scrappy underdogs, chosen heroes?)
- What's the intended emotional register? (Serious, lighthearted, dark, balanced?)

### Content and Comfort
- Gather lines and veils (see above)
- Discuss: violence level, mature themes, horror elements, romance, moral ambiguity
- Establish: will player characters be able to act against each other (PvP)?

### Scheduling and Logistics
- How often do we play, and for how long?
- What happens when someone can't make it — does the session happen without them?
- What platform/location?

### Character and Party
- Do characters know each other at the start, or meet at the table?
- Are character concepts approved individually, or do players build together?
- Is the party expected to cooperate, or can characters work at cross-purposes?

### Expectations
- Is this a GM-driven narrative, player-driven sandbox, or hybrid?
- How much time should players invest between sessions (note-taking, backstory, preparation)?
- What happens to a character if a player leaves the campaign?

## Handling Difficult Behaviors Constructively

Approach disruptive behavior as a design problem, not a character problem. Most difficult behavior has a solvable root cause.

### The Rules Lawyer
Argues rules interpretations frequently, often mid-combat. Slows play. May be right, may be wrong, but the process is disruptive.

**Root cause:** Often feels unsafe when rules are applied inconsistently; finds procedural clarity comforting; may feel disrespected when overruled.

**Constructive response:**
- Establish at session zero that GM rulings stand in the moment and can be discussed after the session.
- Give them a role: "I want you to be our official rules reference. If there's a question mid-session, you look it up and I'll make a ruling based on your finding."
- Take their research seriously when they bring it after the session. Sometimes they're right.

### The Min-Maxer
Optimizes heavily, may feel frustrated if the world doesn't challenge their build, or may overshadow other players in combat.

**Root cause:** Often expresses care about the game through mechanical mastery; the game is satisfying when the machine works.

**Constructive response:**
- Design encounters that are genuinely challenging at their optimization level.
- Create situations where mechanical optimization is secondary (social encounters, mysteries, dilemmas with no "correct" answer).
- Highlight moments where less-optimized characters succeed through creativity. Don't punish the optimizer; just don't make optimization the only winning condition.

### The Dominant Player
Talks over others, makes decisions for the group, roleplays their character's interaction with every NPC.

**Root cause:** Enthusiasm that hasn't found a cooperative outlet; may not notice the effect on others.

**Constructive response:**
- In the moment: redirect gently. "Aldric, you've got a plan. Before you act — Mira, you've been quiet. What does Mira think?"
- Privately: "You clearly love this game, which is great. I need your help making sure [player name] gets space too. Can you count to three after someone else finishes speaking?"
- Give them a formal role that channels the energy: note-keeper, mapper, initiative tracker.

### The Disengaged Player
On their phone, checked out, passive in scenes.

**Root cause:** Content isn't resonating; schedule is wrong; something outside the game is weighing on them; they don't feel invested in their character.

**Constructive response:**
- Check in privately: "Is the campaign hitting for you? Is there something I can do differently?"
- Rebuild investment: ask them about their character's goals and introduce a scene that pursues those goals.
- Address scheduling honestly: a player who's exhausted every session may need to sit out a campaign cycle.

## Encouraging Quiet Players Without Pressuring Them

Some players are naturally reserved. The goal is to create space, not demand performance.

**What works:**
- Ask closed-ended questions that have easy answers: "Does Seren have any reaction to what just happened?" (Yes/No is a valid answer)
- Give advance warning: "Next scene, I'm going to ask Seren what she thinks. Have a think."
- Design moments that play to their backstory so they have natural investment
- Narrate their character's competence so they feel valued even when quiet: "The guards don't notice you — Mira's instincts are flawless."

**What doesn't work:**
- Putting them on the spot in front of the group with open-ended questions
- Calling attention to their quietness ("You haven't said anything — what's your character doing?")
- Making them feel like they're failing at roleplaying

## When to Take a Break

Take a break when:
- The session has run 90+ minutes without one
- Someone signals discomfort (use the break to check in)
- An argument has started at the table
- Momentum has collapsed (confusing plot, mechanical gridlock, player fatigue)
- A player needs a moment after an emotionally intense scene

A 5–10 minute break resets the table, reduces tension, and often produces better play than grinding through. It is never the wrong call.

## Post-Session Feedback

A brief, informal check-in at the end of each session improves the game faster than any other practice.

**Three questions (spoken or written):**
1. What was your favorite moment this session?
2. Is there anything you'd like more of?
3. Is there anything that didn't land for you?

Keep it brief. Keep it optional. Don't defend yourself against critical feedback — write it down, say "thank you," and think about it later.

Over time, this builds a feedback culture where players feel heard and the GM improves consistently. The table that talks about the game after the session is the table that keeps playing together.
